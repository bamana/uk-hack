Talk of the town

By ABQnews Staff
Published: Tuesday, September 8th, 2015 at 12:02am
Updated: Tuesday, September 8th, 2015 at 5:39am

To save lives, try complying with officers

WHY IS IT that all the discussion concerning negative interaction between citizens and law enforcement ignores the very basic facts?

Every violent confrontation between citizenry and law enforcement has two main common denominators. A law was perceived to have been broken and there was a failure to comply with law enforcement directions. Take away either of those factors and everybody goes home safely to their families.

Yes, there are precipitating factors such as diminished mental capacity and the occasional bad cop, but if you’re going to ignore the two most direct causes of deadly police encounters, you shouldn’t even be in the conversation.

I know my ideas don’t fit the politically correct narrative out there and I could care less. PC remedies have nothing to do with saving lives.

GARY LUECK

Albuquerque

Attacks on police are not ‘hate crimes’

ATTACKS ON POLICE were called a “hate crime” by speaker Conrad Candelaria at the New Mexico Business Coalition’s annual Heroes lunch on Aug. 27 . Candelaria, a former APD commander, contended law enforcement officers should be a protected group under hate crime laws.

The Hate Crime Statistics Act of 1990, as modified by the Hate Crimes Prevention Act of 2009, however, define a hate crime as occurring because of the victim’s race, ethnicity, religion, sexual orientation or disability – attributes that are not voluntary as is the occupation of law enforcement.

New Mexico’s law, initially passed in 2003, now covers crimes based on race, religion, color, national origin, ancestry, age, disability, gender, sexual orientation or gender identity.

The United States is a violent nation. For example, a study of 178 countries by Adam Lankford of the University of Alabama of 291 documented mass shootings in 1966-2012 found the U.S. leads the world with 31 percent of the shootings. The single factor that sets the U.S. apart from the other countries is gun availability – the U.S. ranked first in per capita gun ownership.

Violence has made many occupations dangerous, such as teachers, retail workers, and most recently TV journalists, as well as membership in groups protected by hate crime laws, such as the six Sikhs killed in 2012 at a Sikh temple in Oak Creek, Wis., and the nine African Americans killed in 2015 at the Emanuel AME Church in Charleston, S.C.

Frazier Glenn Miller, a former leader of white supremacist groups, is currently on trial in Kansas City, Kan., for first-degree murder of three people in 2014 at the Overland Park, Kan., Jewish Community Center and nearby retirement home, Village Shalom. Unintentionally, the victims were Christians.

DIANNE R. LAYDEN

Albuquerque

How many people must die before we act?

I WATCHED WITH a mix of horror and déjà vu when three people were shot on live TV by a person who never should have had access to a gun.

I sit to write this letter knowing that by the time I finish, three more people will be killed by guns in America. In the hour it takes someone to write an angry rebuttal, seven more people will be shot. It’s time to stop arguing about this issue – there are not two reasonable points of view to hash out.

Let’s stop letting the National Rifle Association dictate the discourse. Instead, demand a vote on universal background checks, insist guns are kept away from the violently mentally ill and domestic abusers. Close loopholes that make it simple to buy an arsenal on the Internet.

Mothers, fathers, students, journalists: your silence will not protect you. Call, email, tweet, visit your elected officials at the state and federal level right now. Join a gun-violence prevention group.

Let’s work together to stop this insanity.

STEPHANIE NAKHLEH

Los Alamos

Kids need options in school cafeterias

WITH THE NEW school year just beginning, parents’ attention is turning to school clothes, supplies and lunches. Yes, school lunches!

In past years, USDA had used our nation’s schools as a dumping ground for surplus meat and dairy commodities. Not surprisingly, one-third of children have become overweight or obese. Their early dietary flaws become lifelong addictions, raising their risk of diabetes, heart disease and stroke.

Gradually, the tide is turning. New guidelines mandated by President Obama’s Healthy, Hunger-Free Kids Act, require doubling the servings of fruits and vegetables, more whole grains, less sodium and fat and no meat for breakfast. A survey released last week shows the guidelines supported by 86 percent of Americans.

Sixty-four percent of U.S. school districts now offer vegetarian options. More than 120 schools, including the entire school districts of Baltimore, Boston, Buffalo, Detroit, Houston, Kansas City, Los Angeles, Oakland, Philadelphia and San Diego have implemented Meatless Monday. Some schools have dropped meat from their menu altogether.

As parents, we need to work with school cafeteria managers and our own children to encourage the availability and consumption of healthy, plant-based school foods.

MICHAEL GRAY

Albuquerque

PNM’s rate increase is a bad investment

PNM RECENTLY filed its newest rate increase. This will be PNM’s fifth rate increase since 2007. We are worried about PNM’s rising rates and the impact it will have on our household budget.

PNM’s rate increase will not only pay PNM back for expensive pollution controls on a 40-year-old coal plant, it will ensure that we keep on paying more and more for capital expenditures at its nuclear plant in Arizona. In the past 20 years, the Palo Verde nuclear plant has required $3 billion of capital expenditures that come from our pockets.

Cost, climate, environment, health – PNM’s coal and nuclear is a terrible idea and it doesn’t make sense that “environmental” groups would sign on to their dangerous proposal.

I understand that folks are tired of fighting PNM, but we just can’t throw away our survival for their profits.

LISA DONAHUE

Santa Fe

PNM made copper theft even worse

WE HAD A copper theft of our electrical lines recently at our business office that shut down four small businesses. We called PNM and a great electrical company immediately.

The electrical contractor showed up very quickly, while PNM had to be cajoled a bit by us letting them know they had an open box and a live transformer. PNM did the repair work to their transformer and the box within a few hours. Our electricians were standing by at the box, ready to work, but PNM locked the box because some magical bureaucratic paperwork was in process but not yet finished.

The paperwork was very quickly finished, but PNM refused to come back and unlock the box for seven hours, forcing the electricians to work diligently until 3 a.m. instead of being able to finish at a decent hour where power could be restored. So one day of production was lost.

Then, the next day, between our electrical company and us, we spent hours on phone and emails begging somebody to turn the power back on. Again there was some magical bureaucratic paperwork that needed to be completed and nobody, but nobody, at PNM seemed to care about doing anything quickly.

Power was finally restored at 7 p.m. on the second day. We were victimized by thieves for the first day, then victimized by a bureaucratic monopoly that doesn’t really care about small business the second day.

I imagine PNM could not find the time to help us because they were just too busy calculating their latest request for a rate increase. PNM does not deserve more money, they do not deserve a rate increase – they need to be broken up.

HUNTER MORTLEY

Albuquerque

For $1M, hunters deserve more access

STATE LAND Commissioner Aubrey Dunn has apparently dropped his demand that New Mexico hunters, trappers and anglers pay $2 million a year to use some 8 million acres of state trust land. He now wants to charge just $1 million – a five-fold increase over the $200,000 fee charged by a long string of predecessors, both Republican and Democrat.

As justification, Dunn has pointed out that Colorado Parks and Wildlife pays more than $830,000 per year for sportsmen’s access to Colorado trust lands, for example. Similar access to Arizona, Idaho, Nevada and Wyoming trust lands, however, is free. Surely there’s a reasonable middle ground.

Dunn disbanded his Sportsmen’s Advisory Committee, so it’s unclear who is giving him advice. But we have repeatedly said New Mexico sportsmen and women are willing to pay more for the right to hunt, fish and trap on state trust lands.

However, we believe we should also see greater benefits. In particular, hunters need more time to scout before hunting seasons begin, and to be able to camp on trust lands rather than drive in and out every day. Currently, we can’t be on trust lands from roughly dusk to dawn, so are often traveling when we should be hunting.

We also need better access. Hunters too often encounter illegally locked gates on state trust land and poorly marked properties, even though grazing lessees are required by law to keep their leased lands open if those are accessible by public road, trail or adjacent public land. And nearly 1 million acres of state trust land is off limits now because it is surrounded by private land. We would want to see a concerted effort by the Land Office to get those lands open.

New Mexico hunters would hate to lose access to the 8 million acres of state trust land that we have been using for generations. But unless sportsmen get more benefit out of the access agreement than we have in the past, we see no reason to pay any more.

JOHN CRENSHAW

President, New Mexico Wildlife Federation

Santa Fe

EDITOR’S NOTE; The State Game Commission recently rejected the $1 million fee and told N.M. Game and Fish Department negotiators to try to get a better deal.

APS employees are citizens, too

ONCE AGAIN, THE issue of public employees serving as state legislators has come up in an op-ed (” ‘Political pay’ at APS is a bad deal,” Aug. 30) by Paul Gessing (president, Rio Grande Foundation) arguing why they shouldn’t be allowed to serve. Here’s a counter argument. We have a citizen Legislature – doesn’t that mean, by definition, that any citizen can participate? If prohibited, wouldn’t the Supreme Court have some interesting things to say about such a prohibition?

Per diem is not pay. It’s dollars to cover lodging and food expenses in Santa Fe. And employee pay – isn’t that a matter between the employer and employee? Some employers are generous and say they support public service. Others may not be able to afford the dollars.

As for conflict of interest, what’s wrong with an APS employee, and part-time legislator, having one vote for more money for schools? That’s a far less troublesome conflict than a legislator-lawyer killing any and all legislation that affects his business, including the now talked about revision of court and bail rules.

CARL SMITH

Albuquerque

NM senators do what’s best for Obama

I AM ASHAMED to admit that Senators (Martin) Heinrich and (Tom) Udall are representing New Mexico in the U.S. Senate.

By supporting the Iran nuclear deal, they certainly are not representing the will of their informed constituents and the best interests of our country. It has been shown time and again that these two sycophants of the president and consummate party hacks will toe the party line on just about everything wanted by the administration. They’ll put out some hyperbole to justify their actions but rarely make a good case for their lousy decisions.

There is more and more coming out about the Iran nuke deal every day to indicate it is a terrible agreement and a sellout to the Iranians for the sake of a feather in (President Barack) Obama’s legacy cap. Everyone, including the president, says we cannot trust the Iranians yet we are willing to free up multibillion dollars that they will use to support worldwide terrorism.

The inspection provisions of the proposed deal are woefully inadequate and, in spite of what Secretary (of State John) Kerry says, will not prevent Iran from pressing on with their nuclear development programs and hiding their activities from U.N. inspectors. Have we forgotten that the Iranians were supporters of the Marine barracks bombing in Lebanon and the Kobar Towers in Saudi Arabia and many other initiatives that took American lives? They still consider us the “Great Satan” and their public enemy No. 1 and regularly organize rallies with “Death To America” as the main theme. This has not changed since they took our embassy employees hostage way back when and there is no indication that it will change.

It would be nice to see Heinrich and Udall for once stand up against the president and do what’s best for our country and our future generations. …

DAVE COULIE

Albuquerque
