Wild West Drift: Intense competition set for Sandia Speedway

By Bob Christ / Journal Staff Writer
Published: Saturday, August 29th, 2015 at 10:35pm
Updated: Sunday, August 30th, 2015 at 12:02am
.......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... ..........

Albuquerque’s Jim Guthrie raced open-wheel cars in the Indianapolis 500 and in many other top events across the country during his career. But when it comes to joy in a race car, he said the sport of drifting is unrivaled.

“It’s a completely different thrill,” the 53-year-old said between practice runs with son Sean during a Thursday morning session on the asphalt track at Sandia Speedway. “Not that Indy isn’t fun, but this is such a kick in the pants. You’re making lots of noise and you’re making lots of smoke.

“What’s an Indy driver do when he wins? He does doughnuts and makes smoke. Here, we get to do doughnuts all day as if we’ve won the race.”

Guthrie will be among approximately 20 to 30 drivers expected for Saturday afternoon’s Vegas Drift Series Pro-Am event, which is the heart of a party weekend at the West Side track. On Saturday evening and from 10 a.m. to 4 p.m. Sunday, drivers from the Albuquerque-based Whiskey Series will have the run of the facility. Overnight, fans and drivers are welcome to camp out and listen to live music.

Sept. 5-6
• Drift Party Weekend: Sandia Speedway, with Vegas Series and Whiskey Series
• Vegas Pro-Am qualifying: 2 p.m. Saturday
• Top 16 tandem competition: 5:30 p.m.
• Whiskey Series (informal): 8:30-11 p.m. Saturday, 10 a.m. to 4 p.m. Sunday.
• Spectator tickets: $15 both days; $25 two-day pass
• Directions: Heading west from the city on I-40, take exit 149, go left over overpass and take frontage road west for 4 miles. Take a left at stop sign and it’s 2 miles to the track.

“It will be an awesome free-for-all for drivers on the track,” said Albuquerque’s Dan Brockett, a pro driver and Whiskey Series chief. “There’s nothing (formal) scheduled, but they’ll put on a good show. I know there will be three to four teams driving at once.”

Said Guthrie: “What I see at autograph sessions is the widest demographic of (race car) followers I’ve ever seen. It’s cool.”

By definition, drifting is when a driver purposely oversteers his car, keeping control while the front wheels are pointing in the oppositie direction of the turn, with tires burning. The sport originated in Japan in the 1970s and took hold in the U.S. States in the past 15 years.

Brockett said the Vegas series also includes events in Las Vegas, Phoenix and Colorado.

In Saturday’s Pro-Am, after individual qualifying, the top 16 drivers will be seeded in a bracket and vie against an opposing driver in a high-speed ballet for the right to advance. The drivers are judged according to their speed, angle, showmanship and the line taken through a corner or set of corners. The follow car attempts to mirror the leader in side-by-side fashion in turns.

“You get style points for looking totally out of control while maintaining control,” Guthrie said of 1,400 horsepower cars that routinely enter corners at 100-plus mph. “Imagine going down the highway (with your tires) at a 60- or 70-degree angle and still going straight. That’s what you strive for. It’s a magical balance between speed and angle.

“If you can rub the fence (with the rear bumper) without messing up your drift, that’s huge style points, too, and the crowd goes crazy. The judge can’t give you a low score if the crowd is erupting.

“The guy on the intercom will try to get the fans into the judging. That’s all part of the show.”

Knowing that, drivers will want to have boisterous cheering sections.

“I think both of my fans will be out there,” Guthrie said, “but hopefully we’ll have about 5,000 out there from Albuquerque to enjoy the Vegas Drift.”

In the head-to-heads, drivers will make two scheduled runs that last about 25 seconds apiece, taking turns as the lead car. Guthrie explains:

“There is some gamesmanship and there’s some strategy,” he said. “The highest qualifier, he leads and the lower qualifier follows. The lead car then wants to try to get away as fast as he can, wide-open on the throttle and spinning the tires. The following car must stay on his door (while sliding into the turns). If the follow car is too far back, he’s in the smoke and can’t see where he’s going and gets lost.

“Then, you return to the starting line and now the follow car leads and you do the same thing. The judges will then pick which car did better and he’s the winner and moves on.”

However, if the drivers split the two runs, it’s a “one more time” for the drivers and they have two more runs (and maybe two more) until a winner is declared. Between the two-run sessions, the cars briefly leave the track for another set of tires.

“You go through tires in about 50 seconds out there,” Guthrie said.

“Luckily I’m in a Falken Tire-sponsored car.”

Among top local drivers expected to compete with Guthrie in the Vegas Pro-Am, according to Brockett, are Jeff Osbourne, Ryan Serna and Gerard Jacinto.

“A lot of these drivers have done pro-ams before and one of them could take the win,” Brockett said. “The biggest difference between the Vegas guys is they have fancier trucks and trailers. There’s not much of a skill difference.”

The winner Saturday moves on to the top-level Formula Drift Series, in which Brockett competes.
