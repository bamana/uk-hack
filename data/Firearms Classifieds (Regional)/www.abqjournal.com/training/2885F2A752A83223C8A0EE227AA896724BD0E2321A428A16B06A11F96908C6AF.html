Petroglyph Monument reaches milestone

By Rick Nathanson / Journal Staff Writer
Friday, June 26th, 2015 at 12:05am
.......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... ..........

A petroglyph on a boulder at the monument. (Courtesy of the Petroglyph National Monument)

Twenty-five years ago, Petroglyph National Monument was formally established, becoming part of the national park system and the only one in an urban setting to offer such a large concentration of Native American rock carvings.

On Saturday, the public is invited to celebrate that milestone at the monument’s visitor center, from 10 a.m. to 2 p.m.

The event will be marked by live music, speakers, refreshments and a photo display.

Diane Souder, the chief of interpretation and outreach at Petroglyph National Monument, said that at the time legislation was signed creating the monument on June 27, 1990, “we thought there were about 10,000 petroglyphs, but to date, we’ve documented more than 24,000.” Most of them were carved between 1300 and 1650 by the ancestors of present-day pueblo Indians, she said.

The pubic is invited to attend the 25th anniversary of Petroglyph National Monument on Saturday, from 10 a.m. to 2 p.m. Celebratory events, including live music, speakers and displays, will be at the monument’s visitor center, 6510 Western Trail NW (at Unser). For further information, contact the visitor center at 899-0205.

Also at the time, the monument, stretching 17 miles along a volcanic escarpment, was considered the far edge of the city.

Today, its 7,236 acres are almost surrounded by development, said Souder, who has been assigned to Petroglyph National Monument since its beginning.

Of 407 “units” in the national park system, Petroglyph National Monument is the only one jointly owned and actively managed by the National Park Service and a municipality â€“ in this case, the city’s Open Space Division, she said.

The unusual arrangement has been highly efficient in terms of visitor services, park maintenance and law enforcement, she said.

According to a National Park Service report, Petroglyph National Monument annually attracts about 116,000 visitors who contribute a cumulative economic benefit to the local economy of $8.3 million.

The visitor center guest book shows that visitors have come from more than 60 countries, and many of those countries have their own petroglyphs left by indigenous people, Souder said. “They come here to see how we manage the park and educate the public to protect our petroglyphs.”
