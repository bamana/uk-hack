The Institute of American Indian Arts reminds how violently the West was settled

By Kathaleen Roberts / Journal Staff Writer
Sunday, August 16th, 2015 at 12:02am
.......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... ..........

“Burn Baby Burn” by Jeffrey Gibson is a repurposed wool army blanket with canvas, glass beads and copper jingles. (Courtesy of the artist and Marc Straus Gallery)

SANTA FE – As the Santa Fe Indian Market swarms flood the city, a meditation on the apocalypse offers some somber reflections at the Institute of American Indian Arts Museum of Contemporary Native Arts.

Curator Candice Hopkins conceived the group show after reading “Blood Meridian,” Cormac McCarthy’s savage indictment of Manifest Destiny. The novel follows a ragtag group of scalp hunters sans ethics or morals who roamed both the Southwest and Mexico for bounty, pleasure and sheer compulsion. For Hopkins, the story mirrors the settlement of the West.

“It’s something people deliberately try to forget,” she said. “I’m very aware of the timing with Indian Market. I was looking to do something that’s powerful and challenging.”

“An Evening Redness in the West” opens Friday, Aug. 21.

The end of the world carries with it the promise of a new one. Ornately beaded gas masks and dystopian landscapes form the remnants of a collapsed society. Intricate drawings of a human-less sci-fi landscape peppered with monsters and otherworldly creatures create the backdrop.

Santa Fe artist Naomi Bebo’s (Menominee/Ho-Chunk) intricately beaded mask reveals its pluralistic function in war, storytelling and spirituality. Native people use masks to build relationships and conduct ceremonies transcending time, place and self-identity, the artist writes in a biographical statement.

“To me, it’s really dark,” Hopkins said. “It’s a representation of the way any new material was brought into our culture and we made it our own. The Plains people beaded guns.”

If you go
WHAT: “An Evening Redness in the West”
WHEN: Friday, Aug. 21, through Dec. 31
WHERE: Institute of American Indian Arts Museum of Contemporary Native Arts, 108 Cathedral Place, Santa Fe
HOURS: 10 a.m.-5 p.m. Mondays through Saturdays. Noon-5 p.m. Sundays
HOW MUCH: $10/adults; half-price for seniors (62+), students with valid ID and New Mexico residents. Free for members, Native people, veterans and their families, youth (16 & under). Call 505-983-1666 or visit iaia.edu/museums

Former Institute of American Indian Arts instructor Norman Akers (Osage) created “Sorting Out Blind Sensations” with a background of charred trees, churning tornados and fluttering crows.

“The main figure is blind, so you don’t know if he’s aware of what’s going on,” Hopkins said.

Akers’ work is in the permanent collections of both the New Mexico Museum of Art and the University of New Mexico Art Museum, as well as MOCNA.

Andrea Carlson’s (Anishnaabe/Ojibwe) massive “Ink Babel” spans 15 by 11 feet in a collage of drawings resembling film stills. Carlson grew fascinated by the late 1970s-early-1980s cannibal film craze.

Many were based on the trope of anthropologists heading into unknown territories to study the natives. Created mostly by Italian filmmakers, they featured graphically gory depictions of primitive, Stone-age natives. Carlson uses cultural cannibalism as a metaphor for the assimilation and consumption of cultural identity.

Joseph Tisiga’s (Kaska Dene First Nation) dreamlike watercolor “Sweetened by False Generosity” addresses myth and identity through a white shaman carrying a totem pole. He roasts something over an open fire fed by ceremonial headdresses and Haida totems. In “The Great Metaphysician,” a top hat-wearing Native the artist dubbed the Red Chief strides through a room decorated by a bear rug.

“It’s about people taking on other identities,” Hopkins said. “They’re kind of surreal.”

Jeffrey Gibson (Choctaw) creates geometric abstract paintings and sculpture drawings on both Native and modernist influences, from 19th-century Iriquois beadwork to ’90s club culture. Gibson created “What We Want What We Need” from a wool army blanket, glass beads, tin jingles, nylon fringe, acrylic yarn, copper coated wire, copper cones, turquoise, amethyst, quartz crystals, plastic sequins, stone arrowheads and nickel seed. Gibson lifted the title from Public Enemy’s 1989 song “Fight the Power.”

The form was inspired by Chilkat weavings and other traditional textiles, robes and shawls found in museum collections. His artwork intermingles traditional Native motifs and materials with contemporary references.

Powow regalia and 19th-century rawhide containers merge with modernist geometric abstraction. Gibson’s work hangs in the permanent collections of the Museum of Fine Arts, Boston, the Smithsonian National Museum of the American Indian and the Denver Art Museum.

“Beaded Mask” by Naomi Bebo has seed beads, deer hide, ermine and ribbons on an Iraqi gas mask. (Courtesy of Diaz Contemporary)

Multidisciplinary Cree artist Duane Linklater’s “Tautology” borrows thunderbird imagery from Anishnaabe Woodland School artist Norval Morrisseau and turns it neon.

Thunderbirds “represent the upper world, but they’re shape-shifters,” Hopkins said. “They’re one of the most forceful beings in the world in the Anishnaabe view. They can cause thunderstorms, all kinds of chaos on earth. There’s a sense that humans aren’t the central force on Earth. We exist around much more powerful beings.”
