Urban wilds

By Tania Soussan / For the Journal
Published: Thursday, March 26th, 2015 at 12:02am
Updated: Thursday, March 26th, 2015 at 5:59am
.......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... .......... ..........

Valle de Oro is not your average national wildlife refuge.

For starters, it’s right in the middle of Albuquerque and the first urban refuge in the Southwest, part of a push by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service to make refuges more accessible to urban areas where 80 percent of the country’s population lives.

The 570-acre refuge in Albuquerque’s South Valley also is unusual in that it’s run by an all-female team of 20- and 30-somethings who are leading site planning, outreach, habitat restoration, community engagement, compliance and environmental education efforts.

Consider the well-documented gender gap in natural resources, land management and the science field in general and it becomes clear how unusual that management team is.

A recent report by the Paris, France-based Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development concluded that “less than one in 20 girls considers a career in science, technology, engineering or mathematics (STEM) compared to one in five boys.”

Refuge Manager Jennifer Owen-White has undergraduate and graduate degrees in the sciences and is working on a doctorate in forestry and natural resource interpretation.

“We certainly don’t have enough women in conservation, especially in these management roles, but I see it improving,” she said, adding that women are particularly skilled at facilitation and building relationships and partnerships.

“That’s really critical to the success of this refuge — and balancing that with really knowing the science,” she said.

The refuge — on land that once was Price’s Dairy farm — sits up against the Rio Grande bosque seven miles south of Downtown Albuquerque.

When she was named a woman of influence by Albuquerque Business First this month, Owen-White told the magazine her greatest professional achievement has been completing the $18.5 million land and water acquisition for the refuge in less than half the time it was expected to take.

The grassy fields and irrigation ditches attract a host of wildlife and birds, particularly ground-nesting birds, grassland species and geese as well as cranes during the winter and wading birds when the ditches are flowing and the fields are flooded with irrigation water. Plans for the refuge include trails, a visitor center and restored native habitats. An environmental assessment with the proposed design should be presented to the public this summer.

For now, the refuge is open from 8 a.m. to 5 p.m. daily. The refuge also hosts monthly open houses and sponsors educational programs at schools and other sites. The next open house from 1 to 4 p.m. on April 25 will feature the Next Generation Conservationist Career Fair with more than 30 natural resource and conservation professionals.

The team of women starting or making their careers at the refuge includes Owen-White and two other U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service employees. Monica Kimbrough is the Middle Rio Grande coordinator for refuges, and Teresa Skiba is Valle de Oro’s environmental education specialist.

Also on the staff are transportation scholar Marisa Rodriguez-McGill and two AmeriCorps Volunteers in Service to America (VISTA) members: Corrie Parrish, who works specifically for the refuge, and Larisa Chavez who is based at Valle de Oro but works on Middle Rio Grande projects. AmeriCorps Native American Water Corps member Julia Bernal also is working for Valle de Oro.

In addition, resident volunteer Kristen Owenreay lives at the refuge and works on full-time community engagement and partnership building.

“We help build each other up, share skills and knowledge,” said Owen-White, who jokes that at 35 she is the “old lady” of the team. “I love the idea of inspiring young women.”

Rodriguez-McGill didn’t start out with a conservation background but her work at Valle de Oro — planning how to link the refuge to trails and bus lines to avoid large parking lots — has inspired her to go on to an environmental science graduate program at Yale University once her position ends next month.

“We’re really growing these female conservationists,” Kimbrough said. “It’s definitely a unique thing.”
